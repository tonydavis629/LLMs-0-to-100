## More Data Is Not Automatically Better

Beyond a point, **what** you train on matters more than how much:

:::columns cols="2" gap="34px"
- **Quality** &mdash; a smaller corpus of clean text can beat a larger pile of boilerplate and spam
- **Diversity** &mdash; many domains and styles generalize better than one repeated genre
+++
- **Deduplication** &mdash; removing near-duplicates improves generalization, even though it removes tokens
- **Domain balance** &mdash; the ratio of code, prose, and academic text shapes what the model is good at
:::

## Side Quest: Memorization vs Generalization

Put one passage in a tiny dataset many times, and the model will spend its capacity **memorizing that exact string** instead of learning a reusable pattern.

:::columns cols="2" gap="34px"
**Memorization**

"The model learned this exact sequence of characters." Verbatim recall of training text &mdash; a privacy and copyright liability, and not a transferable skill.
+++
**Generalization**

"The model learned a pattern it can reuse." The goal of pretraining. This is exactly why we **deduplicate** data and why we watch **validation loss**, which only rewards patterns that transfer.
:::

Duplicated text is the bridge between the two: it quietly turns generalization back into memorization.

## Benchmark Contamination

If evaluation examples leak into the pretraining data, the model can score well by **recall** rather than ability.

- The model may have literally seen the test questions and answers during pretraining
- Its benchmark numbers then overstate its real capability
- This is why deduplication and decontamination try to remove known benchmark text &mdash; and why a suspiciously high score deserves scrutiny

Contamination is easy to introduce by accident (benchmarks are published on the web that gets scraped) and hard to fully rule out.

## Data Mixture Shapes Behavior

The blend of sources is one of the most consequential design choices in a pretraining run:

:::columns cols="3" gap="20px"
**Code-heavy**

More source code improves coding ability &mdash; and, intriguingly, some kinds of structured reasoning.
+++
**Academic text**

Papers and textbooks shift style and broaden factual knowledge.
+++
**Conversational text**

Forums and dialogue change how the model handles turns and informal language.
:::

:::note
Open datasets made this concrete and reproducible. Gao, Biderman, and collaborators at EleutherAI built **The Pile**, an 800GB curated mixture of 22 diverse sources, which became a standard reference corpus for open language-model pretraining.
:::

## Side Quest: The Data Wall

What happens when high-quality **human** text, not model size, becomes the scarce resource?

- The best public text is finite; the largest models already train on much of it
- Responses include aggressive **curation and deduplication**, careful **domain balance**, and increasingly **synthetic data** generated by other models
- This reframes the frontier: not just "can we afford a bigger model?" but "is there enough good data to feed it?"

The data wall is a recurring theme in later modules, and a reason the simple "just scale up" story is incomplete.
